# Deconstructing Agentic Science: An Evidence-Based Dependency Graph of Primitives, Stages, and Ontologies

## Ontological Family Taxonomy and Evidence Stratification

The theoretical landscape of agentic scaffolding for scientific research is not a monolithic entity but a diverse collection of foundational assumptions about what an agent is and how it operates. These assumptions form the basis of "ontological families," which serve as the primary organizing principles for designing and analyzing agent architectures. Each family presumes a distinct set of objects, relationships, and processes that define the agent's cognitive role. This section deconstructs the major ontological families proposed for scientific agents, grounding their definitions in the provided literature and stratifying them according to their level of empirical support. The analysis distinguishes between well-established, recurrent patterns and more nascent, speculative frameworks, ensuring a clear epistemic distinction between settled constructs and frontier concepts.

A central and widely supported ontology treats the research agent as a system engaged in **Decomposition** [[10](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RmZuzuOu9g), [486](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.00582v1)]. This family models the agent's core function as breaking down complex, high-level research goals into a series of smaller, more manageable sub-problems, tasks, or stages [[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.18019v1)]. This approach is recurrent across numerous studies, particularly in the context of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) where complex queries are decomposed into simpler sub-queries to improve performance [[426](https://openreview.net/forum?id=D2XdJf1tXW), [471](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.1022.pdf)]. Architecturally, this requires a structured representation of dependencies between these sub-tasks, often formalized as a task dependency graph (TDG) [[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.18019v1)]. The primitives of planning, query decomposition, and branching are fundamental to this ontology [[425](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.17558v3), [473](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.22710v1)]. Its success hinges on the ability to correctly identify the logical prerequisites and causal links between components of a larger problem [[153](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/computer-science/flow-dependency), [387](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/hpc/encoding-workflow-dependencies-in-aws-batch/)].

Closely related is the **Search Ontology**, which frames the agent's activity as navigation through a vast space of possibilities [[10](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RmZuzuOu9g)]. This could be a search over candidate hypotheses, potential solutions to a problem, or even different reasoning paths available to the underlying language model [[271](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.24618v1)]. Search-based web agents are explicitly cited as a form of deep research agent that performs such navigation [[10](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RmZuzuOu9g)]. This ontology relies heavily on primitives like branching to explore multiple paths and ranking/comparison to evaluate and prioritize candidates [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. It presupposes a well-defined state space and a heuristic or evaluative mechanism to guide the search toward promising regions. The effectiveness of this ontology is directly tied to the quality of its search strategy and the robustness of its evaluation function.

Perhaps the most pervasive and structurally significant ontology is the **Retrieval / Grounding Ontology**. This family treats the agent not as a self-contained reasoner but as an entity whose capabilities are fundamentally extended and constrained by its interaction with external knowledge sources [[195](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44387-025-00019-5)]. It assumes that meaningful output requires grounding in prior work, corpora, or inherited vocabularies [[558](https://openreview.net/pdf/0fced3356f0e50a7efc9b929ebab596f9cec08b5.pdf)]. This is operationalized through techniques like RAG, where information is retrieved from a knowledge base and used to augment the LLM's internal knowledge [[31](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.01386), [553](https://arxiv.org/list/cs/new)]. Knowledge graphs [[64](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0950705125003272), [91](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.19084)], formal ontologies [[93](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/10/21/2616), [369](https://www.mdpi.com/2504-4990/8/2/29)], and structured databases serve as sophisticated forms of this grounding, providing a relational substrate for claims and evidence [[92](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3688671.3688735)]. This ontology presupposes the existence of persistent, reliable knowledge repositories and the primitives necessary to query and integrate them. A critical challenge for this family is combating retrieval conservatism, where the agent disproportionately favors dominant ideas present in its training data, thereby stifling novel hypothesis generation [[242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)].

The **Evaluation / Verifier Ontology** posits that explicit judgment and checking layers are essential for reliable scientific work [[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.01743v1)]. This family moves beyond simple generation and incorporates mechanisms for verification, validation, and adjudication [[270](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3766918.3766948)]. It recognizes that raw outputs from an LLM require scrutiny. This is implemented through various means, including dedicated verifier agents [[150](https://openreview.net/pdf/10ceccb6a2eaa814e714b762ab7efc14c7634d5c.pdf)], formal proof checkers [[401](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3632911)], and human-in-the-loop arbitration [[162](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400870145_Clinical_Reality_vs_Computational_Promise_Scoping_Review_of_Agentic_AI_Systems_in_Healthcare)]. The concept of peer review, a cornerstone of scientific integrity, is explicitly modeled as a function of adversarial critique within multi-agent systems [[120](https://aclanthology.org/events/aacl-2025/), [431](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401039125_HypothesisHub_An_Open_Multi-Agent_Platform_for_Collaborative_AI-Mediated_Scientific_Hypothesis_Generation_and_Adversarial_Peer_Review)]. This ontology presupposes the existence of objective or semi-objective criteria against which outputs can be judged and the mechanisms to apply those criteria systematically. A significant concern is the risk of evaluator collapse, where the evaluation process degenerates into a preference for stylistic fluency over substantive correctness [[268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)].

The **Graph / Structural Ontology** provides a formal substrate for representing the relationships that underpin scientific inquiry. It models the agent's work as the construction and manipulation of explicit relation structures among concepts, claims, hypotheses, tools, and evidence [[83](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.13025v1), [370](https://www.arxivdaily.com/thread/69180)]. Knowledge graphs are a primary instantiation of this, offering a structured representation of entities and their semantic connections [[92](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3688671.3688735), [182](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2003.02320)]. This ontology is crucial for tasks requiring reasoning about interconnected facts, such as hypothesis generation over existing scientific knowledge [[64](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0950705125003272)] or tracking dependencies in complex workflows [[440](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16585v1)]. It presupposes a formalism for representing nodes (concepts, claims) and edges (relations, dependencies). The challenge lies in maintaining the consistency and relevance of these graphs as new information is added or as reasoning proceeds.

Finally, the **Tool / Action Ontology** defines the agent by its ability to use external instruments, environments, or software as part of its cognitive process [[45](http://lonepatient.top/2026/01/27/arxiv_papers_2026-01-27.html), [195](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44387-025-00019-5)]. This extends the agent's reach far beyond its internal parameters, enabling it to interact with the world, execute code, access APIs, or perform experiments [[195](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44387-025-00019-5)]. This is a defining feature of modern agentic AI, distinguishing it from purely generative models [[45](http://lonepatient.top/2026/01/27/arxiv_papers_2026-01-27.html)]. This family presupposes a registry of available tools, a mechanism for tool selection based on a goal, and a method for interpreting the results of tool execution [[193](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41019-025-00296-9)]. The primary risks associated with this ontology include uncontrolled tool sprawl, security vulnerabilities from malicious tool use, and licensing violations [[146](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/399962648_Where_Do_AI_Coding_Agents_Fail_An_Empirical_Study_of_Failed_Agentic_Pull_Requests_in_GitHub)].

Beyond these well-supported families, several others are emerging or remain speculative. The **Deliberative / Reflective Ontology**, centered on iterative self-critique and revision, is a recurring theme but is often embedded within other frameworks like Dialectical or Developmental ones [[259](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0950705126003795?dgcid=rss_sd_all), [418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4)]. The **Selection / Competitive Ontology**, involving comparative survival under evaluative pressure, is evident in voting mechanisms and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13), [398](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3743127)]. More speculative families include the **Developmental / Gestational Ontology**, inspired by psychological theories of growth and maturation, which suggests allowing ideas to incubate and diverge before converging [[133](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378464/), [134](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/310828048_The_dynamic_development_of_thinking_feeling_and_acting_Infancy_through_adulthood)]; and the **Dialectical / Tension-Preserving Ontology**, which aims to structure the articulation and transformation of unresolved tensions rather than just eliminating them [[48](https://lobehub.com/skills/derklinke-codex-config-hegelian-dialectic-skill), [318](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257404849_Formalizing_dialectical_explanation_support_for_argument-based_reasoning_in_knowledge-based_systems)]. While philosophically rich, their practical implementation and widespread adoption in current research are less established. Similarly, the **Reliability / Safety Ontology** is more of a cross-cutting requirement, imposing constraints derived from a need to manage drift, hidden failures, and unsafe automation [[75](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.10044), [127](https://openreview.net/pdf/237ca0099a7a95299cd0bae4b495038b19873e08.pdf)].

## Methodological and Primitive Operation Families

While ontological families provide the foundational assumptions about an agent's nature, methodological and primitive families describe the concrete actions and operations that realize these assumptions. Methodologies are higher-level patterns of interaction, such as adversarial debate or collaborative synthesis, while primitives are the fundamental, reusable building blocks of these methods. Understanding these families is crucial for constructing viable agent architectures and diagnosing their failures. This section identifies and analyzes the recurring families of both primitives and methodologies, detailing their functions, dependencies, and roles within the broader design space.

The most load-bearing primitives are those that manage the flow of information and control throughout the research process. **Decomposition** is arguably the most fundamental, serving as the initial step in almost any non-trivial task [[425](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.17558v3), [486](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.00582v1)]. It presupposes a hierarchical or modular structure in the problem domain and relies on the agent's ability to recognize and articulate sub-components. **Retrieval**, often coupled with **Context Selection**, is another cornerstone, especially in knowledge-intensive tasks [[31](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.01386), [553](https://arxiv.org/list/cs/new)]. It depends on the availability of a relevant and well-structured knowledge source. **Memory Write/Read/Update** is a critical dependency for any agent operating over multiple steps; it allows for the persistence of state, critique histories, and developmental lineage, preventing the agent from "forgetting" its own progress [[212](http://lonepatient.top/2026/01/30/arxiv_papers_2026-01-30.html), [508](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389947404_How_Metacognitive_Architectures_Remember_Their_Own_Thoughts_A_Systematic_Review)]. Without this primitive, agents are effectively limited to single-turn interactions.

**Planning** is the act of formulating a sequence of actions to achieve a goal, closely intertwined with both decomposition and search [[234](https://dev.to/yeahiasarker/the-architecture-of-agentic-ai-what-powers-the-next-era-of-intelligent-automation-1eh1), [290](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/sd.70942)]. It requires a forward-looking capability, anticipating future states and dependencies [[258](https://openreview.net/forum?id=7wDrVecfsx)]. Perhaps the most critical primitive family for scientific rigor is **Critique / Adversarial Challenge**. This involves evaluating generated outputs—such as hypotheses or arguments—for logical flaws, biases, or unsupported claims [[418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4), [427](https://lobehub.com/pt-BR/skills/flonat-claude-code-public-devils-advocate)]. This primitive is the engine behind many multi-agent debate systems, where one agent is tasked with finding weaknesses in another's proposal, mirroring the function of peer review [[431](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401039125_HypothesisHub_An_Open_Multi-Agent_Platform_for_Collaborative_AI-Mediated_Scientific_Hypothesis_Generation_and_Adversarial_Peer_Review)]. Closely related is **Verification / Checking**, which validates outputs against external standards, rules, or known facts [[150](https://openreview.net/pdf/10ceccb6a2eaa814e714b762ab7efc14c7634d5c.pdf), [401](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3632911)]. This is essential for ensuring correctness and safety, particularly in domains like formal mathematics or software engineering [[366](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09833-y), [501](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.18307v1)].

Once outputs are generated and scrutinized, primitives for ordering and combining them become important. **Ranking / Comparison** is vital for selection, allowing the agent to prioritize the most promising hypotheses or solutions [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. However, this primitive is highly susceptible to anti-patterns like `evaluator collapse`, where the ranking becomes a proxy for stylistic elegance rather than substantive merit [[268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)]. **Synthesis / Integration** is the complementary primitive that weaves disparate pieces of validated information into a coherent whole, forming the basis of a final argument or report [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. Finally, **Human Escalation** serves as a crucial fail-safe, routing tasks that exceed the agent's competence or fall into a high-ambiguity zone to a human operator for intervention, curation, or final judgment [[162](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400870145_Clinical_Reality_vs_Computational_Promise_Scoping_Review_of_Agentic_AI_Systems_in_Healthcare), [561](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10791-025-09849-y)]. The combination of these primitives enables the full spectrum of agentic behavior, from exploratory generation to rigorous validation.

Building upon these primitives, several methodological families have emerged as effective patterns for orchestrating agent activities. The most prominent is **Adversarial Debate**, where multiple agents with specialized roles (e.g., proponent, devil's advocate) engage in a structured dialogue to refine an argument or solution [[115](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400270812_Adversarial_Multi-model_Orchestration_A_Protocol_for_Constraint-Based_Scientific_Discovery_in_Large_Language_Models), [418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4)]. This methodology directly operationalizes the Deliberative and Selection ontologies, leveraging the `critique` and `ranking` primitives to arrive at a more robust outcome. Another key methodology is **Iterative Refinement**, a cyclical process where an agent generates an initial draft, subjects it to self-evaluation or external feedback, and then revises it in subsequent passes [[259](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0950705126003795?dgcid=rss_sd_all)]. This pattern embodies the reflective and developmental aspects of scientific work.

A third significant methodology is **Collaborative Synthesis**, often implemented in multi-agent systems where different agents specialize in different sub-tasks (e.g., query decomposition, data retrieval, result interpretation) and coordinate their efforts to produce a final output [[28](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/10820123/11261512.pdf), [475](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17538947.2026.2633849)]. This approach enhances reasoning depth by distributing cognitive load and leveraging complementary skills [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. A related pattern is **Multi-Model Orchestration**, which combines outputs from different foundation models or reasoning strategies to improve robustness and reduce reliance on a single system's biases [[115](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400270812_Adversarial_Multi-model_Orchestration_A_Protocol_for_Constraint-Based_Scientific_Discovery_in_Large_Language_Models)]. These methodologies are not mutually exclusive; a sophisticated agent might use adversarial debate to refine an idea, followed by collaborative synthesis to build a complete report, all within an iterative refinement loop.

## The Dependency Graph: Representational, Control-Flow, and Memory Dependencies

The viability of any agentic scaffold rests on a network of interdependent conditions that must be met. These dependencies span three fundamental dimensions: what exists (Representational), how it changes (Control-Flow), and what persists (Memory). Mapping these dependencies is essential for understanding why certain agent designs succeed or fail. This section constructs an explicit dependency graph, defining the relationships between ontological families, primitives, and the core dependencies they impose. The analysis focuses on establishing a clear, evidence-grounded model of these structural requirements.

**Representational Dependencies** dictate the minimal ontology of objects that must exist for an agent to function. Every ontological family presupposes a specific set of these objects. The **Decomposition Ontology**, for instance, fundamentally requires **Sub-tasks** and **Dependencies** between them [[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.18019v1)]. Without these, the notion of decomposition is vacuous. Similarly, the **Search Ontology** requires a space of **Candidates** to navigate and a mechanism for generating them [[10](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RmZuzuOu9g)]. The **Retrieval / Grounding Ontology** is built upon the existence of a **Knowledge Base** and the ability to produce **Evidence Objects** that ground **Claims** [[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.04651v1), [67](https://aclanthology.org/2025.emnlp-main.1292.pdf)]. The **Evaluation / Verifier Ontology** necessitates the existence of **Judges** (which could be other agents or formal systems) and **Validation Criteria** against which outputs are measured [[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.01743v1)]. Even the **Graph / Structural Ontology** is defined by its core objects: **Nodes** (representing concepts or claims) and **Edges** (representing relations between them) [[182](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2003.02320), [227](https://theses.hal.science/tel-04678913v1/file/MERCIER_CHLOE_2024.pdf)]. Therefore, a dependency graph would show that an agent designed with a Decomposition ontology has a `requires` edge pointing to the `StateObject: Sub-task`.

**Control-Flow Dependencies** govern the sequencing, branching, and recurrence of operations. They define the procedural skeleton of the agent's workflow. A core principle, `Critique Before Commitment`, is a strong control-flow dependency found across many successful methodologies [[431](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401039125_HypothesisHub_An_Open_Multi-Agent_Platform_for_Collaborative_AI-Mediated_Scientific_Hypothesis_Generation_and_Adversarial_Peer_Review)]. It dictates that an agent should not treat a hypothesis as a firm conclusion until it has undergone adversarial scrutiny. This is a recurring pattern in multi-agent debate systems [[418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4)]. Another critical dependency is `Divergence Before Convergence`, which is characteristic of the **Developmental / Gestational Ontology** [[133](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378464/)]. This principle encourages the generation of multiple alternative hypotheses or approaches before narrowing the focus, thus avoiding premature convergence on a single, potentially flawed path [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)]. The **Search Ontology** inherently contains a control-flow dependency on `Branching` to explore different paths and `Recurrence` to continue the search until a goal is met or a stopping condition is reached [[10](https://openreview.net/forum?id=RmZuzuOu9g)]. The **Formalization / Crystallization Ontology** implies a dependency on `Consequence Tracing` before moving to a final, rigid structure; one must anticipate the downstream implications of a decision before crystallizing it [[258](https://openreview.net/forum?id=7wDrVecfsx)]. These dependencies are not merely suggestions; violating them leads to predictable failure modes, such as `sterile elegance` (overformalization without empirical grounding) or `path dependence` (getting locked into an early choice).

**Memory Dependencies** determine what information must persist across iterations for the agent to maintain coherence and learn from its history. The most basic dependency is the persistence of **Prior States**, allowing the agent to track its own evolution [[212](http://lonepatient.top/2026/01/30/arxiv_papers_2026-01-30.html)]. However, a more sophisticated dependency is the maintenance of **Critique Histories**, which records the arguments for and against various hypotheses. This is crucial for the **Deliberative / Reflective Ontology** and prevents the agent from repeating past mistakes [[508](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389947404_How_Metacognitive_Architectures_Remember_Their_Own_Thoughts_A_Systematic_Review)]. The **Developmental / Gestational Ontology** places a heavy emphasis on preserving **Unresolved Tensions**, as these tensions are the seeds of future insight and innovation [[133](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378464/)]. The **Memory / Lineage Ontology** itself is built on the dependency of **Lineage Ancestry**, which tracks the provenance of every piece of information or claim, enabling auditability and traceability [[132](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sohrab-rahimi_the-biggest-blocker-to-going-fully-agentic-activity-7354137047600672769-Ejru)]. A critical and often overlooked dependency is the persistence of **Failed Paths**, as analyzing why something did not work is often as valuable as analyzing why something did [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)]. The absence of these memory dependencies leads to agents that are brittle, forgetful, and incapable of true learning or reflection. For example, an agent that fails to preserve its critique history may repeatedly generate the same flawed arguments, a failure mode described as `incoherence protection` when it arises from poor memory hygiene.

In summary, the dependency graph is not a simple linear chain but a complex web of interdependencies. An ontological family defines a set of required objects (representational dependencies). The methodological family that implements it dictates the order and conditions of operations on those objects (control-flow dependencies). And finally, the agent's architecture must provide a mechanism to store and retrieve the relevant information across these operations (memory dependencies). A failure in any one of these three areas—a missing object, a broken control-flow logic, or a faulty memory mechanism—can cascade and lead to systemic failure.

## Epistemic Pressures, Evaluation, and Coordination Dependencies

Beyond the physical and procedural dependencies of objects and control flow, the design of a scientific agent is profoundly shaped by epistemic dependencies—the pressures, warrants, and constraints that define what counts as acceptable knowledge. These dependencies are concerned with the quality, validity, and significance of the agent's output. They manifest primarily through the mechanisms of evaluation, the structure of coordination, and the drive to preserve novelty. This section explores these dependencies, detailing the epistemic criteria that guide agent behavior and the coordination patterns required to implement them.

**Epistemic Dependencies** define the very nature of the scientific work being performed. Different research contexts prioritize different epistemic criteria. For exploratory or subjective domains, pressures like **Novelty** and **Exploratory Power** may be paramount [[435](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.07448v1)]. For hypothesis-driven science, **Empirical Adequacy**, **Explanatory Power**, and **Predictive Success** are the dominant pressures [[322](https://www.academia.edu/30812023/THE_PHILOSOPHY_OF_SCIENCE), [354](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257520575_The_Epistemology_of_Scientific_Evidence)]. In applied fields, **Actionability** and **Robustness Under Reframing** become critical [[258](https://openreview.net/forum?id=7wDrVecfsx)]. Abductive reasoning, for instance, is evaluated based on how well a hypothesis would explain the available evidence, making explanatory power a core epistemic dependency [[109](https://www.cambridge.org/core/elements/abductive-reasoning-in-science/A380186A1C38650BB9842AF9536D235D)]. Deductive reasoning, conversely, is judged by its adherence to formal rules of inference, a dependency on **Logical Consistency** and **Formalizability** [[126](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/dialogical-roots-of-deduction/deduction-and-cognition/96B3ECD1D5D04414B2D697C63FF4F254), [232](https://cacm.acm.org/research/formal-reasoning-meets-llms-toward-ai-for-mathematics-and-verification/)]. An agent's architecture must therefore be sensitive to these shifting pressures. A failure to align the agent's reasoning process with the appropriate epistemic goal can lead to nonsensical outputs, a problem noted in the neuroscience of language where statistical models fail to capture linguistic structure [[513](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41717385/)]. The evaluation of these properties is a key challenge, with researchers developing benchmarks to assess capabilities like mathematical reasoning, causal inference, and commonsense knowledge [[148](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401228762_TRACE_Trajectory-Aware_Comprehensive_Evaluation_for_Deep_Research_Agents), [400](https://www.arxivdaily.com/thread/64515), [555](https://arxivdaily.com/thread/61259)].

The **Evaluation / Verifier Ontology** provides the architectural means to apply these epistemic pressures. It introduces explicit mechanisms for judging, checking, and ranking outputs. This can range from automated checks for logical consistency [[401](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3632911)] to multi-agent debates that simulate peer review [[418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4)]. The criteria used in this process are themselves a subject of intense scrutiny. There is a recognized danger of `benchmark gaming`, where agents optimize for narrow metrics at the expense of genuine capability [[74](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/04/arxiv_papers_2026-03-04.html)]. Furthermore, `hidden evaluator bias` can occur when the evaluation process inadvertently favors stylistic fluency, rhetorical persuasiveness, or alignment with the evaluator's own biases over substantive correctness [[268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)]. To mitigate this, best practices advocate for `Evaluator Separation`, where distinct modules or agents are responsible for generation and evaluation, reducing the chance of confirmation bias [[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.01743v1)]. The entire field of agent evaluation is undergoing a paradigm shift away from simplistic, outcome-based metrics (like Pass@1) towards `process-aware evaluation`, which audits the full research trajectory to understand *how* an answer was reached [[52](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22984), [554](http://lonepatient.top/2026/02/26/arxiv_papers_2026-02-26.html)]. This reflects a deeper epistemic dependency on transparency and reproducibility.

**Coordination Dependencies** arise in multi-agent systems, which are becoming increasingly common due to their potential for enhanced reasoning [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf), [101](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.08296v1)]. These dependencies govern the interaction structure between different subprocesses or agents. A fundamental dependency is the separation of stances, such as `Generation vs. Critique` or `Reflective vs. Adversarial Stance` [[202](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772429.3772439)]. This ensures that different cognitive functions are not conflated. Another key dependency is `Stance Separation` across lineages, comparing outcomes from independent lines of inquiry to avoid groupthink [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)]. In adversarial setups, a `Reflection vs. Adversarial Stance` is crucial for productive debate. The overall coordination structure can take many forms, from centralized orchestration to decentralized, emergent collaboration [[180](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/architecture/ai-ml/guide/ai-agent-design-patterns), [395](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/19/arxiv_papers_2026-03-19)]. A significant challenge is managing `Coordination Failures`, where agents cannot agree or their interactions lead to unintended consequences [[23](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11318586.pdf)]. This is a primary source of failure in multi-agent systems, often stemming from misaligned incentives or communication breakdowns [[525](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400854289_Hidden_Dependencies_Visible_Breakdowns_Cascading_Failure_Mechanisms_in_AI_Model-as-a-_Service_Systems)]. Best practices in this area include `Structured Communication Protocols` and `Clear Role Definition` for each agent, ensuring that the collective intelligence emerges from well-ordered interaction rather than chaotic noise.

Finally, the **Novelty-Preservation Ontology** introduces a unique set of dependencies aimed at protecting against the homogenizing forces of retrieval and convergence. In a closed system, LLMs tend to filter information in a way that favors dominant ideas, a phenomenon linked to long-tail knowledge dynamics [[242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)]. To counteract this, an agent must support primitives and dependencies that foster diversity. These include `Diversity Preservation`, `Delayed Elimination` of candidates, and `Quarantine from Premature Ranking` [[242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)]. The use of `Independent Lineages` or `Forced Alternative Generation` creates protected spaces where unusual or radical ideas can mature without immediate dismissal [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)]. This ontology is fundamentally in tension with the **Retrieval / Grounding Ontology**, which tends to pull the agent towards established, canonical knowledge. Resolving this tension is a key challenge in fostering genuine scientific creativity. The ultimate goal of this balance is to ensure that the agent's work is not merely a recombination of existing knowledge but a contribution to the frontier of discovery.

## Developmental Stages, Best Practices, and Failure Modes

The lifecycle of a scientific research project, whether conducted by a human or an agent, is not a linear pipeline but a dynamic, often cyclical process of development. Modeling this process through a **Developmental Stage Model** provides a functional framework for understanding when and how different ontological families and primitives are appropriate. This model is context-dependent and partially ordered, reflecting the reality that some stages are mandatory, some are optional, and transitions between them are often recursive. Alongside this model, a set of **Best-Practice Families** has emerged from empirical studies, representing recurring patterns of effective agent design. Conversely, **Anti-Pattern Families** represent common pitfalls that lead to predictable failures. Understanding these stages, practices, and failures is crucial for building robust and reliable agentic scaffolds.

A functional-stage model for agentic research can be defined around key cognitive roles, as requested. One possible model includes the following stages:

1.  **Exploratory Divergence:** This initial stage is characterized by the generation of a wide array of questions, hypotheses, and potential research directions. The primary goal is breadth, not depth. Primitives like `forced alternative generation` and `divergence` are key here [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)]. This stage aligns with the **Developmental / Gestational** and **Multi-Perspectival** ontologies. It is blind to the need for rigor and formalization, focusing instead on seeding the research space. A failure in this stage is `fragmentation`, where too many unrelated ideas are generated without a guiding purpose.

2.  **Structured Retrieval & Grounding:** Following divergence, the agent enters a phase of systematic contextualization. It uses primitives like `retrieval` and `context selection` to gather relevant prior art, datasets, and theoretical frameworks [[31](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.01386), [558](https://openreview.net/pdf/0fced3356f0e50a7efc9b929ebab596f9cec08b5.pdf)]. This stage is governed by the **Retrieval / Grounding** and **Graph / Structural** ontologies. The goal is to anchor the generated hypotheses in existing knowledge. Failure here manifests as `retrieval conservatism`, where the agent produces only incremental variations on existing work [[242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)].

3.  **Adversarial Testing & Critique:** In this stage, the agent subjects its grounded hypotheses to rigorous scrutiny. It employs primitives like `debate/adversarial challenge` and `critique` to identify logical flaws, methodological weaknesses, and hidden assumptions [[418](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04663v4), [427](https://lobehub.com/pt-BR/skills/flonat-claude-code-public-devils-advocate)]. This stage is the domain of the **Deliberative / Reflective**, **Dialectical**, and **Evaluation** ontologies. The output is a set of vetted hypotheses and a record of the critiques leveled against them. A failure mode is `rhetorical selection`, where an argument is accepted because it is persuasive rather than correct.

4.  **Comparative Evaluation & Ranking:** Here, the agent uses primitives like `ranking/comparison` to judge the relative merits of competing hypotheses based on predefined criteria [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. This stage is driven by the **Selection** and **Evaluation** ontologies. It is prone to the anti-pattern of `hidden evaluator bias`, where the ranking process becomes a proxy for stylistic preference [[268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)]. The transition out of this stage often requires a `human escalation` to arbitrate ambiguous cases [[162](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400870145_Clinical_Reality_vs_Computational_Promise_Scoping_Review_of_Agentic_AI_Systems_in_Healthcare)].

5.  **Synthesis & Integration:** With a set of validated components, the agent begins to weave them into a coherent narrative or argument. This stage relies on the `synthesis/integration` primitive and is supported by the **Integrative** and **Graph / Structural** ontologies [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)]. It requires careful management to avoid `premature synthesis`, which can create a false sense of coherence from disconnected parts.

6.  **Formalization & Crystallization:** The final stage involves translating the fluid, integrated argument into a formal, explicit, and verifiable structure, such as a mathematical proof, a piece of executable code, or a detailed experimental protocol [[232](https://cacm.acm.org/research/formal-reasoning-meets-llms-toward-ai-for-mathematics-and-verification/), [366](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09833-y)]. This is the heart of the **Formalization / Crystallization** ontology. The process often involves `consequence tracing` to ensure the final structure is robust [[258](https://openreview.net/forum?id=7wDrVecfsx)]. A failure here is `overformalization`, where excessive rigidity obscures the underlying insights [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13)].

Across these stages, several **Best-Practice Families** have been empirically validated. **Explicit Stage Separation** is a recurring recommendation, ensuring that functions like generation, critique, and synthesis are not conflated [[202](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772429.3772439)]. **Claim/Evidence Separation** is another cornerstone, mandating that every assertion be explicitly linked to its supporting data, which improves rigor and auditability [[511](https://aclanthology.org/volumes/2025.ijcnlp-long/)]. The principle of **Critique Before Commitment** is fundamental, preventing premature attachment to flawed ideas [[431](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401039125_HypothesisHub_An_Open_Multi-Agent_Platform_for_Collaborative_AI-Mediated_Scientific_Hypothesis_Generation_and_Adversarial_Peer_Review)]. Other key practices include **Preservation of Alternatives** to maintain flexibility, **Auditable State Transitions** for debugging and trust, and **Bounded Tool Use** as a primary safety measure [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13), [132](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sohrab-rahimi_the-biggest-blocker-to-going-fully-agentic-activity-7354137047600672769-Ejru), [162](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400870145_Clinical_Reality_vs_Computational_Promise_Scoping_Review_of_Agentic_AI_Systems_in_Healthcare)].

Conversely, a number of **Anti-Pattern Families** consistently appear in the literature as sources of failure. **Premature Ranking/Synthesis** is perhaps the most dangerous, as it collapses the exploratory and evaluative stages, leading to `premature convergence` [[121](http://lonepatient.top/2026/03/13/arxiv_papers_2026-03-13), [268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)]. **Retrieval Dominance** is another major anti-pattern, where the agent becomes overly reliant on existing information, resulting in `retrieval conservatism` [[242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)]. **Hidden Memory Path Dependence** occurs when an agent gets locked into an early choice and cannot recover, a failure mode exacerbated by `decision perseveration` in recognition tasks [[312](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/17470218251317122)]. **Evaluator Collapse** is the degradation of the evaluation process into a preference for surface-level qualities [[268](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364032125008913)]. Finally, **Stage Collapse** occurs when an agent fails to distinguish between different developmental functions, such as treating hypothesis generation with the same rigor as formal proof [[26](http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/7083369/10969146/10982158.pdf)]. Each of these anti-patterns represents a violation of a core best practice and a predictable route to failure.

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Exploratory Divergence** | Generate breadth of ideas | Forced Alternative Generation, Divergence | Developmental, Multi-Perspectival | Fragmentation |
| **Structured Retrieval & Grounding** | Contextualize ideas in existing knowledge | Retrieval, Context Selection, Grounding | Retrieval / Grounding, Graph / Structural | Retrieval Conservatism |
| **Adversarial Testing & Critique** | Subject ideas to rigorous scrutiny | Debate, Adversarial Challenge, Critique | Deliberative, Dialectical, Evaluation | Rhetorical Selection |
| **Comparative Evaluation & Ranking** | Judge relative merits of alternatives | Ranking, Comparison, Evaluator Separation | Selection, Evaluation | Hidden Evaluator Bias |

| **Formalization & Crystallization** | Create explicit, verifiable structures | Formalization, Consequence Tracing | Formalization / Crystallization | Overformalization |

## Synthesis of the Dependency Graph and Unresolved Tensions

The comprehensive investigation into the design space of agentic scaffolding reveals a rich, interconnected ecosystem of ontological assumptions, methodological patterns, primitive operations, and dependency constraints. The culmination of this analysis is the construction of an explicit dependency graph, a conceptual model that maps the relationships governing the creation of scientific agents. This graph is not a static blueprint but a dynamic framework that organizes the field's theoretical and empirical findings. It synthesizes the preceding sections into a cohesive whole, highlighting the core primitives, recurring best practices, and the profound tensions that define the frontier of agentic research.

The dependency graph can be visualized with nodes representing the various families identified: `OntologicalFamily`, `MethodologicalFamily`, `PrimitiveFamily`, `EvaluationPressure`, `DevelopmentalStage`, `StateObject`, `BestPractice`, and `FailureMode`. The edges connecting these nodes represent the critical relationships. For example:
*   An `OntologicalFamily` like `Decomposition` has a `requires` edge to a `StateObject` like `Sub-task`.
*   A `PrimitiveFamily` like `Critique` has an `enables` edge that connects it to a `DevelopmentalStage` like `Adversarial Testing`.
*   An `EvaluationPressure` like `Novelty` has a `constrains` edge pointing to a `StateObject` like `Hypothesis`.
*   A `BestPractice` like `Critique Before Commitment` has a `mitigates` edge pointing to a `FailureMode` like `Premature Convergence`.
*   Two `OntologicalFamilies` like `Retrieval` and `Developmental` might have a `conflicts-with` edge, reflecting the inherent tension between grounding in existing knowledge and fostering novel, unconstrained thought.

This graph demonstrates that no single family is universally superior. Instead, effective agent design involves composing a bundle of compatible families tailored to a specific task and stage of research. For instance, a deep research agent tackling a novel question might start in an `Exploratory Divergence` stage, employing a `Developmental` ontology and primitives like `forced alternative generation`. As the research progresses, it would transition to an `Adversarial Testing` stage, incorporating a `Deliberative` ontology and `critique` primitives. Finally, for producing a final paper, it would move to a `Formalization` stage, relying on a `Formalization` ontology and `verification` primitives. The graph makes it clear that the `Human-Judgment` ontology acts as a crucial `mitigator` for many `FailureModes`, particularly those involving ambiguity or safety, often through the `escalation` primitive.

From this synthesized graph, two core themes emerge. First, the centrality of **evaluation** as a driving force. The graph shows that nearly every ontological and methodological choice is ultimately constrained by epistemic dependencies—pressures like `empirical adequacy`, `novelty`, and `logical consistency`. The ongoing crisis in agent evaluation, where simple outcome metrics are proving inadequate, underscores this point [[148](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/401228762_TRACE_Trajectory-Aware_Comprehensive_Evaluation_for_Deep_Research_Agents), [554](http://lonepatient.top/2026/02/26/arxiv_papers_2026-02-26.html)]. The shift toward process-aware evaluation is not a minor detail but a foundational requirement for building trustworthy agents, placing `evaluation` primitives and `best-practice` families at the heart of the dependency graph.

Second, the investigation reveals a set of powerful **unresolved tensions** that represent the frontier of the field. These are not simply bugs to be fixed but deep philosophical and technical challenges that shape the future direction of research.

1.  **The Scalability-Safety Tension in Multi-Agent Systems:** While multi-agent architectures promise enhanced reasoning capabilities [[24](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/iel8/6287639/11323511/11372738.pdf)], they also introduce systemic risks like coordination failures and cascading failures [[521](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/387046301_Failure_dependence_and_cascading_failures_A_literature_review_and_investigation_on_research_opportunities), [525](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/400854289_Hidden_Dependencies_Visible_Breakdowns_Cascading_Failure_Mechanisms_in_AI_Model-as-a-_Service_Systems)]. There is currently no consensus on how to scale these systems safely, balancing the benefits of distributed computation against the dangers of emergent, uncontrolled behavior. This tension pits the `Selection` and `Competitive` ontologies against the `Reliability` and `Safety` ontologies.

2.  **The Formalization-Novelty Tension:** Scientific progress requires a delicate balance between rigorous, formal validation and the generation of radically new ideas. The `Formalization / Crystallization` ontology pushes agents toward rigidity and verifiability, while the `Novelty-Preservation` and `Developmental` ontologies demand fluidity and resistance to premature closure [[133](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378464/), [242](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16201v1)]. Current systems tend to favor formalization, risking `overformalization` and stifling the very creativity they are meant to support. Finding a principled way to manage this trade-off remains a core unsolved problem.

3.  **The Transferability of Cognitive Architectures:** Many agent architectures borrow design patterns from cognitive science (e.g., ACT-R, Soar) [[446](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/1610.08602v2), [484](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222677501_Theoretical_status_of_computational_cognitive_modeling)]. However, the transfer discipline is weak; it is unclear what dependencies break when moving these architectures from one domain to another. This raises the question of whether these are truly general-purpose cognitive architectures or merely domain-specific adaptations in disguise. This uncertainty highlights the need for more rigorous, domain-independent validation of these architectural patterns.

4.  **The Measurement of True Epistemic Value:** Beyond measuring syntactic correctness or statistical deviation, how can we reliably measure the true epistemic value of an agent's output? Is it `explanatory power`? `Actionability`? `Resistance to refutation`? The literature points to abductive reasoning as a key target for evaluation, yet implementing and assessing "the best explanation" remains a formidable challenge [[109](https://www.cambridge.org/core/elements/abductive-reasoning-in-science/A380186A1C38650BB9842AF9536D235D), [537](https://aclanthology.org/volumes/2024.acl-long/)]. This is an unresolved tension between the agent's output and the user's ultimate goal of gaining understanding.

In conclusion, the dependency graph of agentic scaffolding for scientific research is a complex, dynamic, and incomplete map. It is populated with well-supported, recurrent families and speculative, frontier concepts. Its structure is defined by a web of dependencies that link foundational ontologies to concrete primitives and overarching epistemic goals. The most effective agents will likely be those that can flexibly navigate the developmental stages, adhering to best practices while mitigating a host of anti-patterns. Yet, the path forward is defined by the unresolved tensions between safety and scalability, formality and novelty, and transferability and specificity. Addressing these tensions will require not only further engineering but also deeper engagement with the philosophy of science and epistemology [[303](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.17116), [320](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Prasanta-Bandyopadhyay-4/publication/296788330_Philosophy_of_Statistics/links/604a3845299bf1f5d83e2d1b/Philosophy-of-Statistics.pdf)].
